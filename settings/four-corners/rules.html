Rules for This Setting

All of these rules are open to discussion and, if all players agree, change.

Character Creation

Race. Select a race from the races page. Check with your DM if you want to play a different race.
Background. Select a background from the backgrounds page.
Feat Points. You start with 2 feat points at level 1.
Ability Score Increase. You may increase one score by 2 and another score by 1, or three different scores by 1 each.
Skill Proficiencies. You gain proficiency in any two skills of your choice.
Tool Proficiency. You gain proficiency in one tool of your choice.
Common Language. You can speak, read, and write the common language of the party. Decide what this language is as a group.

Removed Content

Content from Unearthed Arcana is not allowed, especially feats.
The following spells are unavailable:
  • Leomund's tiny hut
  • that's it

Terminology

Adjacent

"Adjacent" means "within 5 feet."

Bloodied

A creature is called "bloodied" if its current hit points is equal to or less than half its hit point maximum. This is the point at which the creature's body shows obvious signs of harm. In general, it is possible to tell at a glance whether a creature is bloodied.

Save Ends

If the words "save ends" appear in parentheses after an effect, the target of the effect can repeat the saving throw at the end of each of its turns. On a success, the effect ends for that creature.

Rules

Also see the glossary page.

Actions

Instead of actions and bonus actions, you have 3 action points (AP) per turn and each action costs a number of them. See action points for an explanation of this system.
See actions for a list of action options available to all characters. Changes from DnD 5e:
  • The Charge action lets you dash and make a single weapon attack, but briefly makes you easier to hit.
  • The Study action codifies thinking during combat.
  • The Search action has been expanded to encompass Survival, Insight, and Medicine.
  • The Taunt action lets you taunt a creature.
  • The Frighten action lets you taunt a creature.
  • The Ready action, when readying a cantrip, does not require concentration.
  • The Magic action subsumes the Cast a Spell action.
  • The Hide action is a Stealth check against passive Perception to establish the hidden condition.
  • The Attack action lets you draw or stow one weapon per attack.

Conditions

See conditions. Changes from DnD 5e:
  • Transformed and concentrating are now conditions, but functionally nothing is different.
  • Grappled scales differently with size and is a saving throw to escape.
  • Grappling is a counterpart to the grappled condition with similar effects.
  • Exhaustion linearly reduces attack rolls, ability checks, saving throws, and spell save DCs. Being knocked out or failing to sleep inflicts 1 level of exhaustion, and 10 levels is fatal.
  • Invisibility grants advantage on initiative checks.
  • Being incapacitated gives disadvantage on initiative checks and prevents speaking.
  • Being hidden is on a per-creature basis and is associated with a Hide DC, determined by the Hide action and contested by passive Perception and the Search action.
  • Being old gives permanent levels of exhaustion.
  • Ongoing damage inflicts recurring damage at the end of each of your turns.
  • The flanked condition gives enemies a +2 bonus to hit when you are surrounded.
  • The taunted condition makes you want to attack a particular creature.
  • The slowed condition slows you down and makes you vulnerable to attacks.
  • The dazed condition is a weaker version of incapacitated, reducing your action points and preventing reactions.

Dropping to 0 Hit Points

When you are reduced to 0 hit points and fall unconscious, you gain a level of exhaustion.

Variant Rules: Injuries

Consider the simple injuries and hardcore injuries variants. These variants are mutually exclusive and replace the Dropping to 0 Hit Points rule.

Feat Points

See feat points for an explanation of the feat point system, and feats for the actual feats available.

Languages

Languages come in many dialects, not all of which are mutually intelligible. See languages for a list of languages and rules for understanding a language you do not know.

Movement

See movement. Changes from DnD 5e:
  • Sneaking allows you to maintain your Hide DC when you move while hidden.
  • Jumping uses a simple formula and either an Athletics check or your passive Athletics.

Resurrection

Resurrection brings a soul back to the material plane. This also provides a means for supernatural creatures to slip in with the revived soul. The DM rolls on the Unwanted Follower table when one of the following spells is cast: revivify, raise dead, resurrection, or true resurrection.

Unarmed Attacks

All unarmed attacks are now simple melee weapons (see the weapons table). Changes from DnD 5e:
  • The unarmed strike deals 1d1 B/P/S damage and can count as a light weapon.
  • The grapple option is an attack instead of an ability contest. The condition itself is also different.
  • The push option is an attack that triggers a saving throw instead of an ability contest.
  • The trip option is an attack that triggers a saving throw instead of an ability contest.
  • The pull attack is the opposite of pushing, for creatures that have a way to extend their reach.

Player Options

There are different player options than normal. Options are still being changed and added. If an option you are currently using changes, you decide whether to continue playing with the old version or update your character to the new version.

Backgrounds

Your background represents your culture and/or training you had prior to becoming an adventurer. It provides a discrete set of features that your character starts with. More nebulously, your background influences ability check DCs, especially where worldly knowledge and lore are concerned.
These backgrounds are completely different from 5e backgrounds and are not interchangeable.

Classes

Your class gives features as you level up, as usual. To a lesser extent than backgrounds, your class may influence ability check DCs, especially the check is directly related to your class's in-world lore.
Notable changes from 5e classes:
  • The fighter gets maneuvers as a base feature, with infinite uses. This version of the fighter is more tactical than the 5e version.
  • The ranger has Hunter's Mark as a built-in feature, and can change its favored enemies and terrain. Many of the subclasses are different and expand on the base class's features.
  • The druid is similar to the 5e version.
  • The monk switches between stances instead of relying on a limited amount of ki. It is much more complex and dynamic than the 5e version.
Ask your DM if you want to play a class (or version of a class) not listed above, such as from 5e, OneDnD, or Rezia.

Equipment

Your equipment determines how you fight, among other things.
Notable changes from 5e equipment:
  • The greatbow is a powerful ranged weapon that requires Strength investment.
  • Shields now have light, medium, and heavy variants.

Feats

Your feats are customizations unique to your character.
Notable changes from 5e feats:
  • Feats are decoupled from class progression and ability score increases:
    • You gain 2 feat points per level (including level 1) to spend on feats.
    • No feat gives an ability score increase.
  • Sharpshooter and Great Weapon Master have been removed.
  • Many new feats are available, and some old feats have been tweaked or redesigned completely.

Races

Your race is your character's species. Aside from the listed set of features, your race may influence other creatures' behavior towards you, for better or worse. Elves and dwarves infamously do not get along and people of all races are wary of tieflings, for example.
Notable changes from 5e races:
  • Your race does not determine your initial ability score increases. The "Ability" racial trait only describes what is typical.
  • Your "Language" racial trait can be changed with the Unusual Upbringing feat. Many backgrounds have language requirements as a proxy for a racial requirement. The Unusual Upbringing feat allows you to circumvent that and adopt a nontraditional background.
  • In general, races do not give proficiencies. Proficiencies represent skill due to knowledge. Instead, some races give situational advantage on ability checks, representing their biological adaptations.
  • Your "Age" racial trait gives your old age threshold and aging rate, which determine how you age.
  • Dragonborn are more dragon-like, and can grow wings (with feats).
  • Dwarves are sturdier and have more subraces: Fjord, Mountain, Hill, Deep, and Cinder.
  • Elves have different subraces: the immortal High and Wood elves, and the mortal Sea, Jungle, and Fey elves.
  • Gnomes get some new features and different subraces: Rock, Rot, Sand, Fey, and Forest.
  • Goblins have subraces: Common, Deep, and the wretched Ungoblin.
  • Goliaths are largely the same mechanically, with an extra trait.
  • Humans are completely different, but still bland. The Ogof subrace has magical powers.
  • Orcs replace half-orcs and have subraces: Cave, Ice, Wood, and Great.
  • Salamanders are an elemental race that just recently emerged from volcanic craters. They have superheated bodies and don't like to get wet.
  • Aasimar, tiefling, and genasi are supernaturally empowered members of other races.
    • Aasimar have two subraces, and unlike other races, have a prescribed alignment: Theurgic (good) and Malefic (evil).
    • Tiefling are literally cursed, but can draw power from their condition. Their subrace determines the nature of their curse, pushing them towards a particular alignment: Atrophic (chaotic) or Leguleian (lawful).
    • Genasi have the usual elemental subraces of Air, Earth, Fire, and Water, but with completely different features.

concentration

Concentration

Some spells and other effects require you to maintain concentration in order to keep them active. If you lose concentration, such an effect ends. You can also choose to end concentration on any effect at any time (no action required).
If a spell must be maintained with concentration, that fact appears in its Duration entry, and the spell specifies how long you can concentrate on it.
Normal activity, such as moving and attacking, doesn't interfere with concentration. The following factors can break concentration:
  • You lose concentration if you invoke another effect that requires concentration. You can't concentrate on two effects at once.
  • Whenever you take damage while you are concentrating, you must make a Constitution saving throw to maintain your concentration. The DC equals 10 or half the damage you take, whichever number is higher. If you take damage from multiple sources, such as an arrow and a dragon's breath, you make a separate saving throw for each source of damage.
  • You lose concentration on an effect if you are incapacitated or if you die.
  • Certain environmental phenomena, such as a wave crashing over you while you're on a storm-tossed ship, may require you to succeed on a DC 10 Constitution saving throw to maintain concentration. This is at your DM's discretion.

Ready

Ready

Cost: varies
Sometimes you want to get the jump on a foe or wait for a particular circumstance before you act. To do so, you can take the Ready action on your turn so that you can act later in the round using your reaction.
First, you decide what perceivable circumstance will trigger your reaction. Then, you choose the action you will take in response to that trigger, or you choose to move up to your speed in response to it. The Ready action costs the same number of AP as the action you ready, or 2 AP if you ready movement. Examples of readying an action include "If the cultist steps on the trapdoor, I'll pull the lever that opens it," and "If the goblin steps next to me, I move away."
When the trigger occurs, you can either take your reaction right after the trigger finishes or ignore the trigger. Remember that you can take only one reaction per round.
When you ready the Magic action to cast a spell, you cast it as normal but hold its energy, which you release with your reaction when the trigger occurs. To be readied, a spell must have a casting time of an action, and holding onto the spell's magic requires concentration unless it is a cantrip. If your concentration is broken, the spell dissipates without taking effect. For example, if you are concentrating on the web spell and ready magic missile, your web spell ends, and if you take damage before you release magic missile with your reaction, your concentration might be broken.

Magic

Magic

Cost: varies (repeatable)
When you take the Magic action, you cast a spell that has a casting time of an action, or you use a feature or a magic item that requires a Magic action to be activated. This use of the Magic action costs a number of AP determined by the spell or magic item.
If you cast a spell that has a casting time of 1 minute or longer, you must take the Magic action on each turn of that casting, and you must maintain concentration while you do so. This use of the Magic action costs 2 AP. If your concentration is broken, the spell fails, but you don't expend a spell slot.
This action is repeatable, but you can only use it to cast one non-cantrip spell per turn.

Obscured

When vision in an area is hindered by smoke, darkness, or a similar phenomenon, the area is obscured. There are two degrees of obscurity.

Lightly Obscured

In a lightly obscured area, such as dim light, patchy fog, or moderate foliage, creatures have disadvantage on Wisdom (Perception) checks that rely on sight.

Heavily Obscured

A heavily obscured area, such as darkness, opaque fog, or dense foliage, blocks vision entirely. Nothing in that area can be seen.

cover

Cover

Walls, trees, creatures, and other obstacles can provide cover during combat, making a target more difficult to harm. A target can benefit from cover only when an attack or other effect originates on the opposite side of the cover.
There are three degrees of cover. If a target is behind multiple sources of cover, only the most protective degree of cover applies; the degrees aren't added together. For example, if a target is behind a creature that gives half cover and a tree trunk that gives three-quarters cover, the target has three-quarters cover.

Half Cover

A target with half cover has a +2 bonus to AC and Dexterity saving throws. A target has half cover if an obstacle blocks at least half of its body. The obstacle might be a low wall, a large piece of furniture, a narrow tree trunk, or a creature, whether that creature is an enemy or a friend.

Three-Quarters Cover

A target with three-quarters cover has a +5 bonus to AC and Dexterity saving throws. A target has three-quarters cover if about three-quarters of it is covered by an obstacle. The obstacle might be a portcullis, an arrow slit, or a thick tree trunk.

Total Cover

A target with total cover can't be targeted directly by an attack or a spell, although some spells can reach such a target by including it in an area of effect. A target has total cover if it is completely concealed by an obstacle.

hidden

Hidden

While you are hidden from a creature, you gain the following benefits:
  • You have a Hide DC, which is typically determined by the Hide action. If you move at least 5 feet on a turn without sneaking, you must reroll the Hide DC by making another Dexterity (Stealth) check.
  • The creature can't see you.
  • The creature can't track your movement.
You cease to be hidden from the creature immediately after any of the following occurrences:
  • You make a sound louder than a whisper.
  • Another creature reveals your location.
  • You make an attack roll or cast a spell with a verbal component
  • You aren't heavily obscured or behind any cover.
  • The creature succeeds on a Wisdom (Perception) check against your Hide DC. The Search action lets a creature attempt this check.
  • Your Hide DC becomes less than the creature's passive Perception.
This condition is typically obtained via the Hide action.

Transformed

Transformed

While transformed into a creature, you experience the following effects:
  • Your game statistics are replaced by the statistics of the creature, including hit points, Hit Dice, and mental ability scores. You retain your alignment and personality. If the creature has any legendary or lair actions, you can't use them.
  • You are limited in the actions you can perform by the nature of your new form. You can't speak, cast spells, or take any other action that requires hands or speech, unless your new form is capable of such actions. Transforming doesn't break your concentration on a spell you've already cast, however, nor prevent you from taking actions that are part of a spell that you've already cast.
  • Your equipment melds into your new form and has no effect.
While transformed into an object, you experience the following effects:
  • Your game statistics are replaced by the statistics of the object.
  • Your equipment melds into your new form and has no effect.
  • You are incapacitated, can't move, and are unaware of your surroundings.
  • You have no memory of time spent in this form.
When you revert to your normal form, you return to the number of hit points you had before you transformed, assuming you didn't die. If you revert as a result of dropping to 0 hit points, and any excess damage carries over to your normal form.

Exhaustion

Exhaustion

This condition is cumulative. Each time you receive it, you gain 1 level of exhaustion. You die if your exhaustion level exceeds 10. While you have at least 1 level of exhaustion, you experience the following effects:
  • When you make an attack roll, ability check, or saving throw, you subtract your exhaustion level from the d20 roll.
  • Subtract your exhaustion level from the spell save DC of any spell you cast.
Finishing a long rest removes 1 of your levels of exhaustion. You gain 1 level of exhaustion whenever any of the following occur:
  • You are reduced to 0 hit points and fall unconscious.
  • You fail to sleep for at least 8 hours minus your Constitution modifier in a 24-hour window.

Jumping

Jump

Jumping lets you clear gaps or obstacles. Distance traveled as part of a jump counts as movement, and like all movement, your total movement on a turn cannot exceed your speed.
When you jump, perform the following series of calculations; don't round until the end. The resulting value is the maximum number of feet you can jump, not counting limitations from your speed.
  1. Start with your passive Athletics. Alternatively, you can make a Strength (Athletics check) and use the result instead.
  2. Divide the value by 2.
  3. If you did not get a running start immediately before jumping, divide the value by 2 again. To get a running start, you must move in a straight line in the direction you want to jump for a number of feet determined by your size, as shown in the Running Start Distances table.
  4. To determine the vertical distance you can clear, divide the value by 2 again.
If you land in difficult terrain, you must succeed on a DC 10 Dexterity (Acrobatics) check or fall prone.
